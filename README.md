# Aviation Accident Analysis
Key Findings
Small aircraft

Makes like Waco, Grumman‑Schweizer, and Maule consistently show the lowest injury fractions and destruction rates.

At the model level, Maule variants (MX‑7‑235, M‑7‑235B, M‑4) and the Grumman‑Schweizer G‑164A stand out with exceptionally low fatal/serious injury fractions.

This confirms that the make‑level safety signal holds true when drilling down to specific aircraft types.

Large aircraft

McDonnell Douglas and Boeing show favorable safety profiles overall.

However, performance varies significantly between models:

MD‑88 and Boeing 737‑300 have very low injury fractions.

Boeing 737‑200 shows much higher injury fractions, highlighting the importance of aircraft generation.

This suggests that recommendations should be model‑specific rather than manufacturer‑wide.

Weather condition

Strongly predictive of severity: accidents in poor weather show higher injury and destruction rates.

Clear weather accidents are less severe on average, reinforcing weather as a key contextual factor.

Number of engines

Multi‑engine aircraft tend to have lower destruction rates, possibly due to redundancy and design robustness.

Injury fractions, however, are more influenced by aircraft type and operational context than engine count alone.

Recommendations
For small aircraft insurers: prioritize Maule and Grumman‑Schweizer models, which show consistently strong safety outcomes.

For large aircraft insurers: highlight McDonnell Douglas MD‑88 and Boeing 737‑300, but avoid blanket recommendations for all Boeing models — generation matters.

General guidance: factor in weather conditions and operational context when assessing accident severity risk.

Caveat: large‑aircraft results are based on smaller samples (687 accidents vs. 64,475 for small aircraft), so findings should be treated as directional rather than definitive.
